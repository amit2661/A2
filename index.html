Dataset Description- Residential sales data in the City of Boston, 2000-2023

This dataset, released as part of the Homes for Profit report by the Metropolitan Area Planning Council (MAPC), provides over 20 years of real estate transaction data in the City of Boston area. It captures information on property sales, ownership patterns, investor activity, and financial details, making it a valuable resource for analyzing speculation and its effects on the housing market.

The key features in the dataset can be categorised as:

  1. Property Characteristics
  2. Transaction Information
  3. Speculation and Investment Indicators
  4. Financial Metrics & Profitability

This dataset gives us useful insights into tracking investor-driven price increases and assessing the impact of cash sales, identifying absentee ownership and mapping speculation hotspots.
However, there are limitations with respect to large number of Null values (intentionally retained in Figure 1), lack of clarity between fields year and yearbuilt and unclear acronyms like RCD in property type. There are also contextual factors such as zoning laws, and investor related nuances which require additional data processing.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How does investor-driven speculation impact long-term homeownership rates in Boston?
    Motivation: After reading the Homes for Profit report, I realised that Boston has seen a decline in % homeownership, particularly among middle- and lower-income residents. Investors who buy and sell homes frequently may be preventing long-term homeownership by keeping properties in constant turnover rather than allowing families to settle. Examining trends in investor ownership versus individual ownership can help determine whether speculative activity is making it harder for residents to own homes and have a decent lifestyle.

  2. Do cash sales contribute to speculation and displacement in Boston’s housing market?
    Motivation: The Indian real estate market greatly slumped in 2016 when demonetisation was introduced. This proved the effect of cash sales (including on my pre-owned property). Seeing the large number of foreign investors from the story Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth, drives me to explore the magnitude of this issue in US (Boston) market.

  3. Are speculative investors targeting certain property types more than others, and what does this mean for housing diversity?
    Motivation: It is important to examine which types of housing are most affected. Are investors mainly flipping single-family homes, multi-family units, or condos in high-rise buildings? If speculation is disproportionately affecting multi-family properties, this could be reducing affordable rental housing stock. Understanding which property types investors target can shed light on how speculation reshapes the housing supply and whether it worsens affordability for renters versus prospective homeowners. This will help plan a solution to the housing crisis better.

  4. How does the presence of corporate and absentee owners correlate with neighborhood-level rent increases and eviction rates?
    Motivation: The high rent for students and residents in the Cambridge area and being part of the affected group, makes me wonder if speculative investors, especially absentee owners and large corporations in the area, may contribute to these trends. By analyzing the overlap between corporate ownership, absentee landlords, and areas experiencing high rent increases or evictions, we can uncover to what extent speculation is driving housing instability.
